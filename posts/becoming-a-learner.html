On Becoming a Woman Who Never Stops Learning

February 12, 2026
Dear curious one,

I once read that Cleopatra spoke nine languages. Not because she had to. Because she wanted to understand every person who stood before her in their own words. That has stayed with me for years.

The most magnetic women I've known in my life — the ones you can't stop listening to at dinner, the ones who make a room feel more interesting just by being in it — they all share one trait. It's not beauty or confidence or charm, though they often have those too. It's curiosity.

They are women who never stopped being students.

The myth of "not being smart enough"

Somewhere along the way, many of us absorbed the idea that learning is something that belongs to a specific type of person. The academic. The intellectual. The woman with the right degree.

This is a lie.

Learning belongs to anyone who is willing to pay attention. You don't need a university to study history — you need a library card and an afternoon. You don't need a tutor to learn a language — you need consistency and thirty minutes a day. You don't need permission to become deeply knowledgeable about something that fascinates you.

The woman who reads widely, thinks carefully, and asks good questions will always be the most interesting person at the table.

How to cultivate a learning life

Read across disciplines

Don't just read within your comfort zone. If you love fiction, pick up a book on astrophysics. If you love self-help, try philosophy. If you love poetry, try economics. The most interesting ideas happen at the intersection of fields. A woman who reads widely thinks differently.

Keep a commonplace book

This is an old practice — centuries old. A notebook where you copy down quotes, ideas, questions, connections. Not organized. Not polished. Just a living record of your intellectual life. Over time, it becomes a portrait of how your mind works. It is one of the most powerful tools for deep thinking I know.

Learn one thing deeply, always

At any given time, have one thing you're studying with real intention. A language. An instrument. A period of history. A scientific concept. Not casually browsing — actually studying. Taking notes. Practicing. Going deeper than the surface. This is what separates a curious person from a learned one.

Teach what you know

The best way to learn something is to explain it to someone else. Write about it. Talk about it at dinner. Share it with a friend. When you teach, you discover the gaps in your understanding. Those gaps are where the real learning lives.

On being a beginner

There is something quietly brave about being a beginner. About walking into a language class at thirty-five, or picking up a paintbrush for the first time at forty, or enrolling in an online course about something you know nothing about.

The ego hates being a beginner. It wants competence, mastery, the comfort of knowing. But the soul? The soul loves it. The soul was made for wonder.

Let yourself be bad at things. Let yourself be slow. Let yourself ask questions that feel obvious. Every expert started exactly where you are. The only difference is that they kept going.

With a book in hand and a heart wide open,
